## What to review

### 1. Hebrew gender agreement in speech output

Hebrew verb conjugations and possessive pronouns change form depending on the grammatical gender of the person being addressed or spoken about. The TTS engine inflects words based on the written text, but written Hebrew without vowel marks (niqqud) is ambiguous — the same spelling can correspond to both the masculine and feminine form.

- Listen for cases where the male speaker (AGG) addresses or refers to the female speaker (VIC) using the **wrong grammatical gender** — specifically, masculine inflections where feminine is required.
- Examples of ambiguous words that are often mispronounced: שלך (should be *shelakh* when addressing a female, not *shelkha*), הלכת (should be *halakht* for female past tense, not *halakhta*), את/אתה confusion, verb suffixes like -ת vs -תה, etc.
- Note every specific instance you catch, with a timestamp if possible, the word as written, and what gender form was used vs. what was correct given the speaker and addressee.

### 2. Voice pitch — absolute level and consistency

- Is the pitch (fundamental frequency / F0) of each voice at a natural level for a Hebrew-speaking adult of the stated age and gender?
- Does the pitch remain consistent across the clip, or does it drift — either rising or falling — as the conversation progresses?
- Are there abrupt pitch jumps between turns that break the sense of a continuous conversation?
- For the female voice specifically: does the pitch feel unnaturally high for a woman in emotional distress, or at any point during the clip?

### 3. Speech rate

- Is the speaking rate natural for a spontaneous Hebrew domestic conversation?
- Hebrew native speakers in informal/emotional speech typically speak at 4–6 syllables per second. Does the TTS output feel significantly slower, faster, or variable in an unnatural way?
- Does the rate change appropriately with emotional intensity (faster and more clipped at high intensity, slower and more measured at low intensity), or is it flat?

### 4. Acoustic quality — metallic / robotic artifacts

- Describe any perceptible TTS artifacts: buzzing, ringing, metallic timbre, robotic cadence, unnatural formant shaping, or any quality that immediately reads as synthetic rather than human.
- Are the artifacts consistent across speakers, or worse for one voice?
- Do the artifacts worsen at higher emotional intensities (louder, faster speech)?

### 5. Emotional expression

- The script is structured with an intensity arc from 1 (calm baseline) to 5 (extreme peak). Does the audio convincingly convey escalating emotional intensity?
- Is there a perceptible difference in emotional tone between intensity-1 and intensity-5 turns?
- Describe the specific emotional qualities that are missing or underdeveloped: are the voices flat, does stress/urgency fail to come through, does distress sound authentic?
- For a domestic violence detection use case, the model needs to learn from audio that contains real vocal stress markers (increased F0 variance, faster rate, creaky/breathy voice quality, louder volume, shorter pauses). Assess how well the TTS output approximates these markers.

### 6. Naturalness of prosody and turn boundaries

- Do pauses between turns feel natural, or are they too uniform/mechanical?
- Does sentence-final intonation (rising/falling at the end of utterances) match the expected Hebrew intonation patterns for statements vs. questions vs. exclamations?
- Are there any cases where the TTS breaks a word or phrase at an unnatural point?

### 7. Overall suitability for ML training data

- Given all of the above, how suitable is this audio as training data for a model that needs to distinguish genuine domestic violence audio from ordinary conversation?
- What is the risk that a model trained on this data will fail to generalize to real human speech?
- Rank the problems you identified from most to least damaging for the ML training goal.
