If you mean fully convincing real-world incident audio with chaotic room acoustics, overlapping speakers, crying, impacts, movement, and ambient context, that is also possible to a degree, but usually by composing multiple tools rather than getting it perfectly from one model. Current systems can synthesize Hebrew speech and also generate or layer sound effects, but realism drops when you need messy, unscripted, forensic-sounding audio rather than clean produced dialogue.  ￼

For Hebrew specifically, the language barrier is no longer the main blocker. Google documents broad voice coverage across many languages and variants, Azure explicitly lists Hebrew (he-IL) as supported, and commercial platforms such as ElevenLabs advertise Hebrew TTS and multilingual voice generation.  ￼

What remains harder is fine-grained acting realism: panic, interrupted speech, strain, sobbing, screams, distance-to-mic changes, phone compression, room echo, incidental noises, and natural overlap. Some platforms now expose stronger emotional/style controls and even “audio tags” or promptable sound-effect generation, which narrows that gap, but a polished result still usually requires post-production and curation.  ￼

Yes. For a bootstrap dataset pipeline, this is feasible now.

The right expectation is not “synthetic data that is evidentiary-grade” but rather synthetic data that is good enough to start model development, pretraining, data-pipeline testing, and ablation work. Hebrew TTS is available in mainstream platforms including Google Cloud and Azure, and current systems also support style/prosody control, multi-speaker SSML, and insertion of prerecorded audio, which is enough to generate a large amount of structured Hebrew scene audio.  ￼

The main limitation is domain gap. Synthetic audio can help a lot early on, but it will not reliably match messy real incidents: phone placement, clipping, overlap, distance, reverberation, crying under speech, sudden movement, muffling through fabric or a pocket, clinic-office acoustics, and device-specific compression. Research on synthetic audio pipelines keeps finding that this gap remains a central issue, even when synthetic data is very large.  ￼

So for both projects, I would frame the synthetic program like this:

What synthetic data is good for

It is well suited for:
	•	training first-pass classifiers for coarse event detection,
	•	building and debugging the full ingestion/labeling/training/evaluation stack,
	•	stress-testing models across many controlled nuisance variables,
	•	pretraining representations before later fine-tuning on actor-recorded and then carefully governed real data.  ￼

It is not sufficient by itself for:
	•	final operating-threshold selection,
	•	claims about real-world false alarm rates,
	•	forensic-grade robustness,
	•	legality-sensitive “proof capture” assumptions.
Those stages will need later actor data and then tightly governed real deployment data.

Recommended dataset strategy

Build a parameterized scene generator, not just a pile of generated clips.

Each generated example should be produced from:
	1.	a script graph,
	2.	a speaker rendering layer,
	3.	an acoustic scene layer,
	4.	a label layer.

That gives you reproducibility and lets you regenerate whole datasets when you improve assumptions.

1) Script graph

Create fictional Hebrew scene templates with slots for:
	•	relationship type,
	•	aggression intensity,
	•	target phrases,
	•	interruption patterns,
	•	escalation/de-escalation,
	•	bystander presence,
	•	silence gaps,
	•	background context.

For She-Proves, the scene unit should usually be a multi-minute episode with sparse critical moments.
For Elephant in the Room, the scene unit should more often be a front-desk / office interaction with a shorter escalation window.

2) Speaker rendering layer

Use Hebrew TTS for the spoken backbone, varying:
	•	male/female/older/younger-sounding voices,
	•	speaking rate,
	•	pitch,
	•	loudness,
	•	speaking style,
	•	interruption timing,
	•	partial-word truncation,
	•	filled pauses and disfluencies.

Azure SSML explicitly supports controlling voice, style, role, pitch, rate, volume, multiple voices in one document, and insertion of prerecorded audio; Google’s newer Chirp 3 HD voices also emphasize realism and emotional resonance.  ￼

3) Acoustic scene layer

Do not rely on TTS realism alone. Post-process into plausible device audio:
	•	phone near/far,
	•	phone in hand / bag / pocket / on table,
	•	room impulse responses,
	•	clinic office / waiting room / home kitchen / bedroom / hallway,
	•	TV/radio/appliances/traffic,
	•	speech overlap,
	•	compression,
	•	clipping,
	•	AGC-like level pumping,
	•	partial occlusion.

For this, tools like Scaper are useful for structured soundscape synthesis, and pyroomacoustics is useful for room impulse response and propagation simulation.  ￼

4) Label layer

Generate labels at multiple granularities:
	•	clip-level binary labels,
	•	event intervals,
	•	speaker turns,
	•	aggression type,
	•	distress indicators,
	•	severity band,
	•	“actionable alert” vs “non-alert”,
	•	uncertainty flags.

This is important because your two projects probably should not optimize for exactly the same task.

Separate the two projects early

Do not build one shared label schema and assume it fits both.

She-Proves

This sounds closer to:
	•	long-form passive audio,
	•	very low base-rate incidents,
	•	a need for high recall on early-warning cues,
	•	later review and optional preservation.

So the early modeling tasks are probably:
	•	incident-window detection,
	•	distress/aggression cue detection,

Elephant in the Room

	•	shorter interactions,

So the early tasks are probably:
	•	imminent aggression / attack-risk detection,
	•	escalation forecasting over short horizons,
	•	panic/noise/shouting/impact event fusion,
	•	“alert now” policy modeling.

What to synthesize first

Start with three layers of realism, not one:

Tier A — clean synthetic dialogue

Pure Hebrew dialogue with two to three speakers, minimal background noise.
Purpose: get the NLP/audio backbone working.

Tier B — dramatized acoustic scenes

Same scripts, but with room acoustics, overlap, crying/shouting variants, background TV, office chatter, doors, chairs, footsteps, phone placement effects.
Purpose: robustness training.

Tier C — adversarial hard negatives

Emotionally intense but non-target scenes:
	•	excited family arguments that de-escalate,
	•	sports yelling,
	•	loud children,
	•	TV drama,
	•	Hebrew radio call-ins,
	•	animated clinic conversations,
	•	social-worker encounters with agitation but no attack,
	•	crying from non-violence contexts,
	•	laughter that acoustically resembles screaming.
This tier matters a lot for false positives.

AudioSet’s ontology and labels show that classes such as screaming and crying/sobbing exist as distinct acoustic phenomena, which can help when designing coarse auxiliary tasks and hard-negative sets.  ￼

A concrete generation stack

A practical 2026 stack could be:
	•	Script generation / templating: internal LLM plus hand-authored constraints.
	•	Hebrew speech synthesis: Azure and/or Google; optionally evaluate ElevenLabs too for expressiveness in Hebrew.  ￼
	•	Background/noise assets: licensed/open libraries plus your own safely recorded benign environments; MUSAN is a standard open corpus for speech/music/noise and is useful for augmentation.  ￼
	•	Scene compositor: Python pipeline around ffmpeg/pydub/torchaudio + Scaper.
	•	Room/device simulation: pyroomacoustics + simple codec/channel corruption.
	•	Metadata/labels: JSONL + RTTM/TextGrid-like intervals + clip manifests.
	•	Evaluation: stratified by scene family, voice family, room family, distance, overlap, noise, and script source.

The most important design principle

Treat synthetic generation as domain randomization, not realism theater.

You do not need every clip to sound perfectly real. You need the distribution of nuisance factors to be wide and deliberate:
	•	many voices,
	•	many acoustic conditions,
	•	many pacing patterns,
	•	many interruption types,
	•	many non-target confounders.

That is exactly the use case where synthetic pipelines can be genuinely valuable. DCASE work on synthetic sound-event data and Scaper-style soundscape generation both support this general direction.  ￼

My recommendation for your teams

For the next few months, I would explicitly position the synthetic dataset as:

Phase 0 / Phase 1 training data
	•	enough to build prototypes,
	•	enough to compare architectures,
	•	enough to identify promising labels/tasks,
	•	not enough to establish deployment performance.

That framing will keep the teams from overclaiming while still letting them move fast.

Minimal roadmap

A sensible first roadmap would be:
	1.	Define a shared audio scene spec and a project-specific label spec.
	2.	Build 50–100 Hebrew scene templates per project.
	3.	Render 5,000–20,000 Tier A/B clips per project with controlled randomization.
	4.	Create a deliberately large hard-negative set.
	5.	Train baseline models for:
	•	binary alerting,
	•	event segmentation,
	•	auxiliary scream/cry/shout/distress tasks.
	6.	Evaluate by synthetic slices, then freeze the pipeline.
	7.	Later, add actor recordings as a new domain, not as replacements.
